Cell Theory / Cell Doctorine

  • all living things are composed of 1 or more cells
  • cells can maintain vitality independent of one another
  • cells can only arise from other cells

Basic Properties of cells

  • Cells are enclosed by a physical barrier
  • Contain DNA
  • Acquire and utilize energy
  • Carry out chemical reactions
  • Can produce more of themselves
  • Engage in mechanical activities
  • React to stimuli
  • Capable of self regulation (prevent overgrowing, overproducing of specific molecules)

Exceptions of Cell Theory

  • Viruses
    - Bits of nucleic acid that have protein coat
    - intert outside cells
    - reproduce only in cells
  • Viroids
    - circular RNA without protein coat
    - Like parasites of Viruses
  • Prions - proteinaceous infectious particles
    - no nucleic acids
    - capacity for self-assembly and self-duplication

Two Fundamentally Different Classes of Cells

Prokaryotes

  • Always single cell organisms
  • 1 - 10 micro (can be bigger)
  • DNA lies free in cell or nucleoid region (naked DNA)
  • DNA is associated with fewer proteins

Eukaryotes

  • All cells of multicellular organisms are eukaryotes
  • 10 - 100 micro
  • DNA is organised into a nucleus
  • nucleus is an organelle (prokaryotes don't have this property)
  • DNA is associated with a characteristic set of proteins
  • Have network of internal membranes (prokaryotes don't have this property)
    - rough endoplasmic reticulum (ribosomes bound to the membrane network)
    - smooth endoplasmic reticulum (stores calcium)
  • Have cytoskeleton (prokaryotes don't have this property)
    - microtubules (pipe like cylinders 20 - 25 nm diameter)
    - microfilaments (cylinders around 5 nm diameter)
    - intermediate filaments (cylinders or fibers 10 nm diameter)
  • Complex cilia and flagella
  • Capacity for endocytosis and phagocytosis

Common Features

  • Plasma membrane
  • Genetic info encoded in DNA
  • Similar mechanisms for transcription and translation
  • Shared metabolic pathways
  • Similar apparatus for conservation of chemical energy as ATP

Prokaryote kingdoms

  • Archae (Archaebacteria)
    - Extremeophiles
    - Have cell walls
  • Bacteria (Eubacteria)
    - All have cell walls except mycoplasma

Eukaryote kingdoms

  • Protista
    - One celled and some colonial eukaryotic organisms
    - Algae, protozoa
  • Fungi
    - Mulicellular or singlecelled organisms with cell walls
    Depend on external source of organic compounds
  • Plantae
    - Mulicellular organisms with cell walls
    - Have plastids
    - Large vacuoles
    - Carry out photosynthesis
  • Animalia
    - Mulicellular organisms
    - Depend on external source of organic compounds

Biological molecules

  • Organic molecules (contain carbon)
  • Hydrocarbons (contain only carbon and hydrogen)
  • Most organic molecules in biology are hydrocarbons with some hydrogen atoms replaced by a functional group

Functional group

  • Grouping of atoms that behave as a unit
  • Give organic molecules their properties
  • Ester bonds: between carboxylic acid and alcohols (fats)
  • Amide bonds: between carboxylic acid and amines (proteins)

Four Macromolecules

  • Lipids
  • Carbohydrates
  • Nucleic acids
  • Proteins
  • Carbohydrates, nucleic acids, and proteins are all polymers
  • Polymers are composed of large number of monomers

Lipids

  • Small organic molecules insoluble in water but soluble in nonpolar organic solvents
  • ie. chloroform, benzene
  • Hydrophobic or contain significant hydrophobic regions
  • Source of energy in diet and store energy in body
  • Some hormones are lipids
  • Many vitamins are lipids
  • Basic structural elements of biological membranes are lipids

Fatty acids

  • Unbranched hydrocarbon chains with a carboxyl group at one end
  • Chains are usually 14 - 20
  • Chain is hydrophobic
  • Carboxyl group is hydrophilic
  • Fatty acids are amphipathic
  • Form micelles in water
  • Can be saturated or unsaturated
    - Saturated: all carbon atoms of fatty acid chain are joined by single covalent bonds
    - Unsaturated: 1 or more double bonds present between carbons in the chain

Triacylglycerols

  • Store energy for animals and plants
  • Glycerol esterified to 3 fatty acids
  • Fatty acids can be identical or different (mixed fat)
  • Oil: fats that are liquid at room temperature, contain unsaturated fatty acids

Phosphoglycerides

  • Major component of membranes
  • Glycerol esterified to 2 fatty acids
  • Third OH group bonded covalently to phosphate group, parent compound: phosphatidic acid
  • Amphipathic and always have a polar region
  • Form a bilayer (hydrophobic bonds)

Steroids

  • Include cholesterol which is found in membranes
  • Cholestrol needed for synthesis of sex hormones
  • Glucocorticoids

Carbohydrates

  • Have general formula (CH2O)n
  • includes simple sugars (monosaccharides)
  • oligosaccharides (2 - 10 monosaccharides)
    - Attached to lipids forms glycolipids
    - Attached to proteins forms glycoproteins
  • Polysaccharides (long chain of monosaccharides)

Monosaccharides

  • Energy source and carbon source for other celluar compounds
  • Carbon chain containing one aldehyde or ketone plus (-OH) groups
  • Generally 3 - 7 carbon atoms
  • Most molecules are in heterocyclic ring structures
  • Very few are in an open chain

Disaccharides

  • Two linked monosaccharides
  • Covalent bond linking is glycosidic bond

Polysaccharide

  • Glycogen
    - Principal food reserve in animals and fungi
  • Starches
    - Principal food reserve in plants
    - Comes in amylose and amylopectin
  • Cellulose
    - Insoluable, rigid polymer
    - Makes up cell walls of plants
    - Cellobiose is repeating unit of cellulose
  • Chitin
    - Outer covering of insects, spiders, crustaceans
  • Glycosaminoglycans
    - Found in extracellular matrix of animal tissue
    - Can be found in cartilage

Nucleic Acids

  • Macromolecules constructed as long chain of nucleotides
  • Nucleotides consist of 3 units: nitrogen, pentose, phosphate

Nitrogen Bases

  • Two broad types
    - pyrimidine: 3 main bases: cytosine, uracil, thymine
    - purine: 2 main bases: adenin, guanine

Pentose Sugar

  • 5 carbon sugar
  • Either ribose or deoxyribose

Nucleosides

  • a purine or pyrimidine attached to a sugar
  • if pentose is ribose, then ribonucleoside
  • if pentose is deoxyribose, then deoxyribonuceloside

Nucleotides

  • Nucleoside plus phosphoric acid
  • Monomeric units from which DNA and RNA are made
  • Second messengers in cell signalling
  • Agents in energy-transferring reactions during metabolism
  • Coenzymes
    - Nonprotein substances required for enzyme action
    - Adenosine nucleotides combined with vitamins of B complex
  • Ribonucleic Acid (RNA): Chain of ribonucleotides
  • Deoxyribonucleic Acid (DNA): Chain of deoxyribonucleotides

Proteins

  • Consist of one or more polypeptide chains
  • Polypeptide is a polymer of amino acids linked together by peptide bonds
  • Can be:
    - Enzymes: protein catalysts
    - Structural elements
    - Contractile elements
    - Control activity of genes
    - Transport material across membranes
    - Carriers
    - Hormones
    - Antibodies
  • Organic acids that contain an amino group and an R group
  • R group - side chains of variable structure

Peptide Bond (Amide Linkage)

  • Links alpha-amino group of one amino acid with alpha-carboxyl gourp of adjoining amino acid
  • linkage in dipeptides and in polypeptides
  • R groups not involved

Primary Protein Structure

  • Sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide
  • In polypeptide chain amino acids are residues
  • N-terminus end of polypeptide with free alpha-amino group
  • C-terminus end of polypeptide with free alpha-carboxyl group

Secondary Protein Structure

  • Results from hydrogen bonding between oxygen of one peptide group with nitrogen of another
  • R groups not involved
  • Two common structures:
    - Alpha helix: cylindrical, twisting spiral
    - Pleated sheets or beta-bleaded sheets (Polypeptide chains of pleated sheets stretched out and either parallel or antiparallel)
  • 60% of polypeptide chain in average protein exist in these structures
  • Remainder in random coils and turns

Tertiary Protein Structure

  • Way that regions of secondary structure are oriented w.r.t each other
  • Tertiary structure predominates in globular proteins
  • Results from side chain interactions

Motifs vs. Domains

  • Motifs
    - Substructure found among different proteins
    - Recurring combinations of secondary structure
    - Characteristic combination of alpha helices, beta sheets, and loops
  • Domains
    - Found in large proteins
    - Region within protein that folds and functions semi-independently
    - Modules of tertiary structure

Quaternary Protein Structure

  • Most protein have multiple polypeptide chains
  • Chain is a subunit
  • Protein with 2 identical subunits is homodimer
  • Protein with 2 nonidentical subunits is heterodimer
  • Quarternary structure is spatial arrangement of subunits
  • Interactions between R groups hold proteins together in quarternary structure
  • Types of interaction:
    - ionic bonds
    - hydrogen bonds
    - hydrophobic bonds
    - disulphide cross linkage

Fibrous vs. Globular Proteins

  • Fibrous Proteins
    - Highly elongated shape
    - Outside living cells
  • Globular Proteins
    - Compact shape
    - Inside living cells
