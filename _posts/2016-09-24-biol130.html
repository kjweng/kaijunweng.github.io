Cell Theory / Cell Doctorine

  • all living things are composed of 1 or more cells
  • cells can maintain vitality independent of one another
  • cells can only arise from other cells

Basic Properties of cells

  • Cells are enclosed by a physical barrier
  • Contain DNA
  • Acquire and utilize energy
  • Carry out chemical reactions
  • Can produce more of themselves
  • Engage in mechanical activities
  • React to stimuli
  • Capable of self regulation (prevent overgrowing, overproducing of specific molecules)

Exceptions of Cell Theory

  • Viruses
    - Bits of nucleic acid that have protein coat
    - intert outside cells
    - reproduce only in cells
  • Viroids
    - circular RNA without protein coat
    - Like parasites of Viruses
  • Prions - proteinaceous infectious particles
    - no nucleic acids
    - capacity for self-assembly and self-duplication

Two Fundamentally Different Classes of Cells

Prokaryotes

  • Always single cell organisms
  • 1 - 10 micro (can be bigger)
  • DNA lies free in cell or nucleoid region (naked DNA)
  • DNA is associated with fewer proteins

Eukaryotes

  • All cells of multicellular organisms are eukaryotes
  • 10 - 100 micro
  • DNA is organised into a nucleus
  • nucleus is an organelle (prokaryotes don't have this property)
  • DNA is associated with a characteristic set of proteins
  • Have network of internal membranes (prokaryotes don't have this property)
    - rough endoplasmic reticulum (ribosomes bound to the membrane network)
    - smooth endoplasmic reticulum (stores calcium)
  • Have cytoskeleton (prokaryotes don't have this property)
    - microtubules (pipe like cylinders 20 - 25 nm diameter)
    - microfilaments (cylinders around 5 nm diameter)
    - intermediate filaments (cylinders or fibers 10 nm diameter)
  • Complex cilia and flagella
  • Capacity for endocytosis and phagocytosis

Common Features

  • Plasma membrane
  • Genetic info encoded in DNA
  • Similar mechanisms for transcription and translation
  • Shared metabolic pathways
  • Similar apparatus for conservation of chemical energy as ATP

Prokaryote kingdoms

  • Archae (Archaebacteria)
    - Extremeophiles
    - Have cell walls
  • Bacteria (Eubacteria)
    - All have cell walls except mycoplasma

Eukaryote kingdoms

  • Protista
    - One celled and some colonial eukaryotic organisms
    - Algae, protozoa
  • Fungi
    - Mulicellular or singlecelled organisms with cell walls
    Depend on external source of organic compounds
  • Plantae
    - Mulicellular organisms with cell walls
    - Have plastids
    - Large vacuoles
    - Carry out photosynthesis
  • Animalia
    - Mulicellular organisms
    - Depend on external source of organic compounds

Biological molecules

  • Organic molecules (contain carbon)
  • Hydrocarbons (contain only carbon and hydrogen)
  • Most organic molecules in biology are hydrocarbons with some hydrogen atoms replaced by a functional group

Functional group

  • Grouping of atoms that behave as a unit
  • Give organic molecules their properties
  • Ester bonds: between carboxylic acid and alcohols (fats)
  • Amide bonds: between carboxylic acid and amines (proteins)

Four Macromolecules

  • Lipids
  • Carbohydrates
  • Nucleic acids
  • Proteins
  • Carbohydrates, nucleic acids, and proteins are all polymers
  • Polymers are composed of large number of monomers

Lipids

  • Small organic molecules insoluble in water but soluble in nonpolar organic solvents
  • ie. chloroform, benzene
  • Hydrophobic or contain significant hydrophobic regions
  • Source of energy in diet and store energy in body
  • Some hormones are lipids
  • Many vitamins are lipids
  • Basic structural elements of biological membranes are lipids

Fatty acids

  • Unbranched hydrocarbon chains with a carboxyl group at one end
  • Chains are usually 14 - 20
  • Chain is hydrophobic
  • Carboxyl group is hydrophilic
  • Fatty acids are amphipathic
  • Form micelles in water
  • Can be saturated or unsaturated
    - Saturated: all carbon atoms of fatty acid chain are joined by single covalent bonds
    - Unsaturated: 1 or more double bonds present between carbons in the chain

Triacylglycerols

  • Store energy for animals and plants
  • Glycerol esterified to 3 fatty acids
  • Fatty acids can be identical or different (mixed fat)
  • Oil: fats that are liquid at room temperature, contain unsaturated fatty acids

Phosphoglycerides

  • Major component of membranes
  • Glycerol esterified to 2 fatty acids
  • Third OH group bonded covalently to phosphate group, parent compound: phosphatidic acid
  • Amphipathic and always have a polar region
  • Form a bilayer (hydrophobic bonds)

Steroids

  • Include cholesterol which is found in membranes
  • Cholestrol needed for synthesis of sex hormones
  • Glucocorticoids
